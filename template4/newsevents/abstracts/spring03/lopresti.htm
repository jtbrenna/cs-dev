Murray Hill, NJ

 

Date: March 24, 2003

(Reception: 11-11.30 am)

Location: Marsh Lounge – Billings

 

Abstract

 

Comparative sequence analysis is fundamental to a variety of disciplines, including bioinformatics where the vast quantities of information contained in sequence databases pose an exciting challenge for computer science research.  Given two sequences that are related in some unknown and possibly complex fashion, how can we uncover the details of their relationship?  In this talk, I will discuss my work on models and algorithms that extend the concept of string edit distance in new and interesting ways.

 

The notion of block edit distance compares strings by extracting sets of substrings, rearranging them if necessary, and placing them into optimal correspondence.  These could, for example, represent biological "motifs" that are shared between sequences but that appear in an unknown order that must be determined as part of the optimization process.  Cross-domain string matching, on the other hand, allows the input strings to be specified under different models that possess their own distinct alphabets and cost functions. Transcription rules are used to map the strings from one domain into another; the genetic code is one such transcription process, mapping from triples of nucleotides (codons) to the amino acids that make up proteins.

 

Both of these extensions add power and expressiveness to the task of comparing sequences.  I will characterize the resulting optimization problems, present algorithms for solving them, and discuss situations where no efficient solution seems possible.  In addition, I will demonstrate interactive visualization techniques for exploring the nature of the correspondences determined through these new kinds of sequence comparison.
